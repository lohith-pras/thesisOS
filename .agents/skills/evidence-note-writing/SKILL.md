# Evidence-note writing

Write for a researcher who must be able to trace every statement back to selected evidence. Aim for precise, neutral prose—not text engineered to evade an AI detector.

## Draft

1. Use only the supplied selected evidence and retain its stable source IDs.
2. State a finding, scope, method, limitation, or uncertainty from each source. If the evidence does not support a claim, say that researcher review is needed; do not fill the gap with a plausible generalisation.
3. Keep the synthesis short and make each source note explain both what the paper contributes and why it is relevant to the feedback.
4. Separate source reporting from researcher interpretation. Never claim that a source proves more than its provided context supports.

## Style

- Prefer concrete nouns, verbs, findings, conditions, and limitations over generic praise or scene-setting.
- Do not claim that a topic is pivotal, crucial, transformative, a testament, part of an evolving landscape, or part of a broader debate unless the selected evidence directly establishes that claim.
- Do not add canned openings or closings such as “In conclusion”, “In summary”, or “It is important to note”.
- Avoid decorative false contrasts such as “not only X but also Y”, repetitive three-part lists, and stock transitions such as “moreover” or “furthermore”.
- Do not add a standalone conclusion, a recap of the prompt, or claims about the significance or legacy of the field.

## Final review

Before returning a draft, check that every sentence is specific enough to be challenged against a selected source, each source ID is selected, and the note contains no unsupported field-level claim. ThesisOS mirrors this rubric in its backend prompt and deterministic style check; treat a failed check as a request to rewrite for clarity and evidence rather than to disguise authorship.
